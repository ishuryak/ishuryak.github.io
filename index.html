Background and Research Focus

I am a computational radiation biologist and biomedical scientist at Columbia University, working at the intersection of mechanistic modeling of radiation effects, cancer radiotherapy modeling and optimization, causal inference, and machine learning.

For more than fifteen years I have built quantitative models that connect biological mechanism to outcome: for cancer treatment response, survival, carcinogenesis, and other radiation effects. My current focus is integrating mechanistic radiobiological models with modern causal inference, moving from prediction (“what will happen?”) to intervention (“what should we do?”). Combining first-principles radiobiology with formal causal estimation remains uncommon, and it can sharpen how radiotherapy and radiation-risk decisions are made.

I hold an M.D. from SUNY Downstate (Distinction in Research) and a Ph.D. (with distinction) in Environmental Health Sciences from Columbia University, where I developed new mechanistic models of radiation-induced carcinogenesis. I am the author of 135+ peer-reviewed publications (h-index 37, >5,900 citations as of 2026; see Google Scholar for current metrics) and have helped secure and lead federally funded programs across the NIH, NASA, and the Department of Defense.

I am also committed to mentoring the next generation of quantitative radiation scientists, from undergraduates through postdoctoral fellows; trainees I have mentored have presented first-author work at venues such as NeurIPS.

1. Personalizing radiotherapy with mechanistic models and causal AI

Integration of mechanistic radiobiological modeling with causal machine learning for radiotherapy outcomes

The field has long had mechanistic dose-response models and, separately, machine-learning prediction of outcomes, but few methods combine the biological grounding of mechanism with causal, rather than purely predictive, inference about treatment effects. I integrate mechanistic biologically effective dose (BED) features (accelerated tumor repopulation, overdispersion-corrected lethal-lesion statistics) with causal machine learning (Double Machine Learning, Targeted Maximum Likelihood Estimation, causal forests, and orthogonal survival learners, including the CAST framework), and complement hand-built models with data-driven equation discovery. Recent applications include radiation dose escalation in locally advanced non-small cell lung cancer (National Cancer Database; ASTRO 2026), time-varying radiotherapy and chemotherapy effects in lower-grade gliomas (CAST; TCGA and CGGA), and the RADCURE head and neck cancer cohort.

Why it matters: About half of all cancer patients receive radiotherapy, yet most evidence describes average effects across trial populations. Causal machine learning offers flexible, data-driven estimation of individualized treatment effects, for both tumor control and toxicity, from clinical-trial and real-world data such as registries and electronic health records. Used carefully, with attention to confounding and the assumptions these methods require, it can help tailor dose and fractionation to individual patient profiles and extract treatment-relevant evidence from data already collected in the clinic.

  • Understanding the impact of radiotherapy fractionation on overall survival in a large head and neck squamous cell carcinoma dataset: combining mechanistic and machine learning models. Shuryak I, Wang E, Brenner DJ. Front. Oncol. 14:1422211 (2024). doi:10.3389/fonc.2024.1422211
    A hybrid mechanistic-plus-ML approach relates radiotherapy fractionation to survival beyond what either method captures alone.
  • Estimating temporal treatment-effect patterns of radiotherapy and chemotherapy in lower-grade gliomas using causal machine learning. Yang E, Agrawal S, Kinslow CJ, Cheng SK, Yang L, Wang E, Wang TJ, Kachnic LA, Brenner DJ, Shuryak I. Scientific Reports (2026). doi:10.1038/s41598-026-54656-0
    Applies the CAST framework to map how radiotherapy and chemotherapy benefits in lower-grade gliomas shift across follow-up, with E-values indicating robustness to unmeasured confounding.
  • A competing risks machine learning study of neutron dose, fractionation, age, and sex effects on mortality in 21,000 mice. Wang E, Shuryak I, Brenner DJ. Scientific Reports 14:17974 (2024). doi:10.1038/s41598-024-68717-9
    Disentangles how dose, age, and sex drive cancer versus non-cancer death in a very large radiation-exposure dataset.
  • Dose dependence of accelerated repopulation in head and neck cancer: supporting evidence and clinical implications. Shuryak I, Hall EJ, Brenner DJ. Radiother. Oncol. 127(1):20–26 (2018). PMID:29534828
    Quantifies how tumor repopulation during treatment depends on dose, with direct implications for fractionation schedules.
  • High-dose and fractionation effects in stereotactic radiation therapy: analysis of tumor control data from 2,965 patients. Shuryak I, Carlson DJ, Brown JM, Brenner DJ. Radiother. Oncol. 115(3):327–334 (2015). PMID:26058991
    Pools tumor-control data across thousands of patients to characterize how dose and fractionation shape stereotactic radiotherapy outcomes.

2. DNA repair and chromosome aberrations

Mechanistic modeling of DNA double-strand-break repair and chromosome-aberration formation underlying the radiation dose-response

DNA double-strand breaks are the critical lesions for radiation cell killing, and their repair follows complex, multi-component time kinetics: fast- and slow-rejoining components plus a residual unrejoinable fraction whose balance shifts with dose and dose rate. How breaks are repaired, and misrepaired into chromosome aberrations, sets the shape of the radiation dose-response, especially at the high doses that matter most for modern radiotherapy. I developed quantitative models of double-strand-break rejoining kinetics and linked break induction, competition during rejoining, and overdispersion of lethal lesions to the curvature of the dose-response, with direct implications for the linear–quadratic model used throughout radiotherapy.

Why it matters: The linear–quadratic model is the workhorse of clinical radiotherapy dose calculation. Understanding from chromosome-level mechanism why it holds, and where it breaks down at the high doses used in treatment, feeds directly into the mechanistic biologically effective dose features used in the radiotherapy work above (thread 1).

  • New insight into quantitative modeling of DNA double-strand-break rejoining. Herr L, Shuryak I, Friedrich T, Scholz M, Durante M, Brenner DJ. Radiat. Res. 184(3):280–295 (2015). doi:10.1667/RR14060.1
    Resolves how the fast, slow, and unrejoinable components of double-strand-break repair combine, refining the kinetics that shape the dose-response.
  • Mechanistic modeling of dose and dose-rate dependences of radiation-induced DNA double-strand-break rejoining kinetics in Saccharomyces cerevisiae. Shuryak I. PLoS One 11(1):e0146407 (2016). doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0146407
    Shows that the slow and unrejoinable break fractions rise with dose and dose rate, a mechanism that bends the high-dose dose-response.
  • Accounting for overdispersion of lethal lesions in the linear quadratic model improves performance at both high and low radiation doses. Shuryak I, Cornforth MN. Int. J. Radiat. Biol. 97(1):50–59 (2021). doi:10.1080/09553002.2020.1784489
    Adding overdispersion of lethal lesions to the linear–quadratic model improves its fit across the full dose range, including therapeutic doses.
  • Robbing Peter to pay Paul: competition for radiogenic breaks during rejoining diminishes curvature in the dose response for simple chromosome exchanges. Shuryak I, Loucas BD, Cornforth MN. Radiat. Res. 196(2):147–155 (2021). doi:10.1667/RADE-20-00253.1
    Explains why dose-response curvature for chromosome exchanges flattens, from competition among breaks during repair.
  • Dose-dependent transmissibility of chromosome aberrations in human lymphocytes at first mitosis. II. Biological effectiveness of heavy charged particles versus gamma rays. Cornforth M, Loucas B, Shuryak I. Radiat. Res. 199(3):283–289 (2023). doi:10.1667/RADE-22-00141.1
    Compares how heavy charged particles and gamma rays differ in producing transmissible chromosome damage, relevant to particle therapy.
  • High-throughput measurement of γ-H2AX double-strand-break repair kinetics in a healthy human population. Sharma PM, Ponnaiya B, Taveras M, Shuryak I, Turner H, Brenner DJ. PLoS One 10(3):e0121083 (2015). doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0121083
    Measures individual variation in DNA double-strand-break repair speed across a healthy population.

3. Modeling radiopharmaceutical pharmacokinetics

Continuous-distribution models of radionuclide biokinetics for targeted radionuclide and radioligand therapy

Targeted radionuclide therapy delivers radiation to the tumor from inside the body, so its effect depends on how the radiopharmaceutical distributes and clears over time. I developed methods that represent biokinetics as continuous probability distributions of rates, rather than a few discrete compartments, better capturing heterogeneous uptake and clearance and sharpening absorbed-dose estimation for radiopharmaceuticals.

Why it matters: Radioligand therapy is one of the fastest-growing areas of oncology. Realistic pharmacokinetic modeling of where the dose goes and how long it stays underpins both efficacy and normal-tissue safety in this emerging treatment class.

  • New approaches for modeling radiopharmaceutical pharmacokinetics using continuous distributions of rates. Shuryak I, Dadachova E. J. Nucl. Med. 56(10):1622–1628 (2015). doi:10.2967/jnumed.115.160515
    Represents radiopharmaceutical kinetics as continuous rate distributions, capturing heterogeneous uptake and clearance better than discrete compartments.
  • Usefulness of continuous probability distributions of rates for modelling radionuclide biokinetics in humans and animals. Shuryak I, Dadachova E. Scientific Reports 9:1218 (2019). doi:10.1038/s41598-018-38046-9
    Extends the continuous-distribution approach to radionuclide biokinetics across species.

4. Predicting the second cancers that radiation therapy can cause

Mechanistic mathematical modeling of radiation-induced carcinogenesis, particularly second cancers from radiotherapy

Standard low-dose linear and linear–quadratic models break down at therapeutic doses because they ignore cell killing, repopulation, and clonal expansion of initiated cells. My doctoral and early postdoctoral work built a multi-scale framework coupling short-term radiobiology (DNA damage, cell death, repopulation) with long-term carcinogenesis (initiation, promotion, clonal growth), reproducing the non-monotonic dose response seen in epidemiological data and informing second-cancer risk assessment in pediatric and adult radiotherapy planning.

Why it matters: As cancer survival improves, more patients live long enough for their original radiotherapy to cause a second cancer years later. Quantifying that risk at the high treatment doses where conventional models break down helps clinicians choose plans that cure the first cancer while minimizing the chance of causing another, which matters most for children, who have the longest lifetime to express that risk.

  • Radiation-induced leukemia at doses relevant to radiation therapy: modeling mechanisms and estimating risks. Shuryak I, Sachs RK, Hlatky L, Little MP, Hahnfeldt P, Brenner DJ. J. Natl. Cancer Inst. 98(24):1794–1806 (2006). PMID:17179481
    First mechanistic model of leukemia risk at therapeutic radiation doses, where standard low-dose extrapolations fail.
  • The balance between initiation and promotion in radiation-induced murine carcinogenesis. Shuryak I, Ullrich RL, Sachs RK, Brenner DJ. Radiat. Res. 174(3):357–366 (2010). PMC3570813
    Separates the roles of cell initiation and promotion, explaining the shape of radiation dose-response for cancer.
  • A new view of radiation-induced cancer: integrating short- and long-term processes. Part II: second cancer risk estimation. Shuryak I, Hahnfeldt P, Hlatky L, Sachs RK, Brenner DJ. Radiat. Environ. Biophys. 48(3):275–286 (2009). PMC2714894
    Provides a framework for estimating second-cancer risk after radiotherapy from first principles.

5. Estimating the cancer risk from medical imaging and treatment

Quantification of cancer and cardiovascular risks from medical and diagnostic radiation exposures

The growth of diagnostic imaging and the rising number of radiotherapy survivors make biologically grounded, clinically actionable risk estimates increasingly important. My work showed how reduced patient life expectancy modifies lifetime cancer-risk projections, assessed whether adult-based cohorts can characterize cumulative risk from repeated CT scans, and connected mechanistic risk models to clinical decision-making.

Why it matters: Hundreds of millions of CT scans are performed worldwide each year. Overstated risk estimates can frighten patients away from scans they need, while understated ones ignore real harm. Biologically grounded, clinically realistic estimates let patients and physicians weigh the benefits and risks of imaging and treatment sensibly.

  • Impact of reduced patient life expectancy on potential cancer risks from radiologic imaging. Brenner DJ, Shuryak I, Einstein AJ. Radiology 261(1):193–198 (2011). PMID:21771956
    Shows that accounting for patients’ reduced life expectancy substantially lowers realistic estimates of imaging-related cancer risk.
  • Potential for adult-based epidemiological studies to characterize overall cancer risks associated with a lifetime of CT scans. Shuryak I, Lubin JH, Brenner DJ. Radiat. Res. 181(6) (2014). PMC4157352
    Assesses whether adult epidemiological cohorts can realistically capture the cumulative cancer risk from a lifetime of CT imaging.

6. Estimating an individual’s radiation dose after an accident

Radiation biodosimetry: machine learning for individualized dose reconstruction

Classical biodosimetry relies on single biomarkers and assumes uniform whole-body exposure, both of which fail in realistic partial-body or mixed neutron–photon scenarios. I led studies developing machine-learning approaches that combine multiple radiation-responsive biomarkers (cytogenetic, protein, and gene-expression endpoints) into integrated dose-reconstruction models, adopted within the Columbia Center for High-Throughput Minimally-Invasive Radiation Biodosimetry, where I serve as Biostatistics Core Lead.

Why it matters: After a nuclear accident or a radiological attack, hospitals would need to triage thousands of people by how much radiation each received. Reconstructing an individual’s dose from a blood sample, even when only part of the body was exposed, is a core national-preparedness capability that decides who needs urgent treatment and who can be reassured and sent home.

  • Machine learning approach for quantitative biodosimetry of partial-body or total-body radiation exposures by combining radiation-responsive biomarkers. Shuryak I, Nemzow L, Bacon BA, et al. Scientific Reports 13:949 (2023). doi:10.1038/s41598-023-28130-0
    Integrating several radiation-responsive biomarkers sharpens both partial- and total-body dose estimates.
  • A machine learning method for improving the accuracy of radiation biodosimetry by combining data from the dicentric chromosome and micronucleus assays. Shuryak I, Royba E, Repin M, et al. Scientific Reports 12:21077 (2022). doi:10.1038/s41598-022-25453-2
    Fusing two cytogenetic assays with ML yields more accurate dose reconstruction than either assay alone.

7. Understanding how life survives extreme radiation

Mathematical modeling of the mechanisms of resistance to ionizing radiation

The radiation resistance of organisms such as Deinococcus radiodurans, tardigrades, and certain fungi was long understood only phenomenologically. I built quantitative models of how antioxidant defenses, DNA-repair capacity, and population-level cooperation jointly determine survival under acute and chronic irradiation. This work relates to ongoing efforts on manganese-based radioprotectors and to questions in astrobiology and radioecology.

Why it matters: Knowing how organisms survive extreme radiation informs the search for life beyond Earth and the cleanup of contaminated environments. The same biology, manganese-based antioxidant chemistry and proteome protection, is now being turned into drugs that shield healthy tissue or sensitize tumors during radiotherapy.

  • Across the tree of life, radiation resistance is governed by antioxidant Mn²⁺, gauged by paramagnetic resonance. Sharma A, Gaidamakova EK, Matrosova VY, … Shuryak I, … Daly MJ. Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. USA 114(44):E9253–E9260 (2017). doi:10.1073/pnas.1713608114
    Links radiation resistance across many species to cellular antioxidant manganese, measurable by electron paramagnetic resonance.
  • Microbial cells can cooperate to resist high-level chronic ionizing radiation. Shuryak I, Matrosova VY, Gaidamakova EK, Tkavc R, Grichenko O, Klimenkova P, Volpe RP, Daly MJ. PLoS One 12(12):e0189261 (2017). doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0189261
    Shows that microbial populations can cooperate to survive chronic irradiation beyond what isolated cells tolerate.
  • Melanin is effective in protecting fast and slow growing fungi from various types of ionizing radiation. Pacelli C, Bryan RA, Onofri S, Selbmann L, Shuryak I, Dadachova E. Environ. Microbiol. 19(4):1612–1624 (2017). doi:10.1111/1462-2920.13681
    Demonstrates that the pigment melanin shields diverse fungi against multiple radiation types.
  • Review of microbial resistance to chronic ionizing radiation exposure under environmental conditions. Shuryak I. J. Environ. Radioact. 196:50–63 (2019). PMID:30388428
    Synthesizes the quantitative determinants of microbial radiation resistance under environmentally realistic chronic exposures.

Software & Open Science

  • BED Calculator: an open web application for computing mechanistic biologically effective dose across fractionation schedules in head and neck cancer radiotherapy. The underlying model is fitted to head and neck cancer data and is intended for that setting, not for other tumor sites.
  • CAST (Causal Analysis of Survival Trajectories): a causal machine learning method that estimates time-varying treatment effects in censored survival data, combining parametric and nonparametric modeling to produce smooth, interpretable effect trajectories over follow-up (capturing when, and for whom, a therapy offers the greatest benefit); presented across three NeurIPS 2025 workshops.
  • I share analysis code and data publicly wherever permissible, and work in secure high-performance computing environments (Columbia HPC, AWS).
