Why finishing radiotherapy on time matters

About half of all cancer patients receive radiotherapy, and the dose is almost never given all at once. It is split into fractions over several weeks, which spares normal tissue. But tumors are not passive during those weeks. After a lag of roughly four weeks, surviving tumor cells begin to repopulate, and they do it fast enough to eat into the dose being delivered. Every extra day of treatment then costs tumor control.

The biologically effective dose (BED) converts any schedule into a single comparable number. Drag the sliders and watch the penalty appear.

Why you cannot just add up the ions

An astronaut on a Mars mission is not hit by one kind of radiation. Galactic cosmic rays are a mixture: mostly protons, some helium, and a small but disproportionately damaging tail of heavy ions such as silicon and iron. We can only test one ion at a time in the laboratory. So the central question in space-radiation risk is: given what each ion does alone, what does the mixture do?

The obvious answer, add up the effects, is wrong. Not approximately wrong. It gives an answer that is physically impossible, and the explorable below shows exactly how.

Two ways to say "how much worse is this radiation?" RBE asks what dose of γ rays would do the same damage. RER asks, at the same dose, how much more damage this radiation does. They converge toward a common limit as the dose falls, and they close again at high dose. In between they diverge several-fold, and that in-between is precisely the range an astronaut occupies. Drag the dose slider from left to right and watch the gap open and then close again.

The absurdity, in one line. Simply adding the ions predicts excess tumors per mouse for the Mars mixture. But the mixture is mostly protons, and the most damaging component it contains, silicon, would produce only tumors if the entire dose were silicon. Simple additivity predicts a mixture that is worse than being hit by nothing but its own worst ingredient. The correct theory, incremental effect additivity, predicts , which falls sensibly between the individual ion responses. The two answers differ by a factor of 2.6.

The dose-response curves rise steeply and then bend. That bend is the non-targeted effect: cells that were never hit still respond, to signals released by cells that were. It saturates quickly, which is why relative biological effectiveness is enormous at low dose and modest at high dose, and why laboratory data taken at high dose cannot be extrapolated down to the doses astronauts actually receive.

T is the targeted (direct-traversal) term and N the non-targeted plateau, reached when every susceptible cell has responded to the signal. s = 38.66 Gy⁻¹ (95% CI 38.34–38.98) is the saturation rate, and it is shared by every radiation type. The background B and the variance term q are not free: they are derived from the fitted overdispersion parameter r. RBE has no closed form here (the exact solution needs the Lambert W function), so it is solved numerically.

When does a treatment actually help?

A clinical trial usually reports one number: the average benefit. But treatment effects move over time. A therapy can help enormously in year one and not at all by year five, or do nothing early and everything late. A single hazard ratio flattens all of that into a point, and if the effect is not constant, that point can be misleading.

CAST (Causal Analysis for Survival Trajectories) estimates the whole trajectory: how the benefit of a treatment rises, peaks, or reverses across follow-up. Below, the truth is known, because the data are simulated. That is the only setting in which you can actually check whether a method is right.

Why the dose response is curved

The linear-quadratic model is the workhorse of clinical radiotherapy: it is how every dose and fractionation schedule gets compared. It assumes lethal damage lands on cells at random, like raindrops, so the number of lethal lesions per cell follows a Poisson distribution.

Cells are not identical, though. If radiosensitivity varies from cell to cell, the lesion count is overdispersed, and the survival curve stops being what the textbook says. Drag the heterogeneity slider and watch the high-dose tail lift away from the classical curve.

Iron ions are a straight line; gamma rays bend. Curvature in the dose response comes from damage that needs two separate radiation tracks to interact. A single iron-ion track is so densely ionizing that it does the whole job by itself, so its dose response has no curvature at all. This is why the linear-quadratic model, which was built for X-rays, cannot simply be carried over to particle therapy.

Set r to zero and the blue curve collapses onto the classical grey one, which is the textbook linear-quadratic model. Raise r and the high-dose tail lifts: a heterogeneous population always contains resistant cells, and at high dose those are the only ones left. This matters precisely where radiotherapy operates.

Show the math
μ = α·D + β·D² mean lethal lesions per cell SF = 1 / [1 + μ·r]^(1/r) overdispersed (negative binomial) SF = exp(−μ) classical, recovered as r → 0

A negative binomial is what you get when the Poisson mean is itself gamma-distributed, which is exactly what happens when radiosensitivity varies between cells. So r is not a fudge factor: it is the variance of the cell population.

Published fits: DU145 α = 0.16, β = 0.028, r = 0.126. U373MG α = 0.08, β = 0.047, r = 0.241. CHO AA8 α = 0.18, β = 0.019, r = 0.000 (this line really is Poisson, and the honest thing is to show it). Independent cytogenetic estimates in human lymphocytes give r = 0.138 (95% CI 0.020 to 0.301).

Why more dose is not always more cancer

Radiotherapy cures cancer with doses of 50 to 70 Gy. Radiation also causes cancer. So a natural fear is that treatment doses must cause enormous numbers of second cancers, since the standard low-dose models say risk climbs in proportion to dose.

They are wrong, and the reason is that at therapeutic doses radiation is also killing the cells it might otherwise have transformed. Risk rises, peaks, and comes back down. But there is a twist: fractionating the dose lets the surviving cells repopulate between treatments, which pushes the peak out to exactly the doses used in the clinic.

The grey dashed line is what a linear no-threshold extrapolation predicts. The orange curve is a single acute dose. The blue curve is a fractionated course, where cells repopulate between fractions. Fractionation does not just shift the peak, it raises it: repopulation rescues pre-malignant cells that a single large dose would have sterilized.

Why this matters for a real patient. A linear extrapolation from atomic-bomb-survivor data overstates the peak risk severalfold at therapeutic doses. That is not a license for complacency, because the risk it overstates is still real and still substantial, which is itself the surprise: the model predicts that excess risk remains large even at 40 to 50 Gy, and the epidemiology of second cancers agrees. The clinical decision is a trade-off, not a formality, and it matters most for children, who have the longest time left to express the risk.

Show the math
ERR = (Q1·Q2 + Q3)/Q4 − 1 Q1 = (1 + Y·D) / [1 + Y·D·(1 − exp(−δ·Ty))] promotion Q2 = [(exp(b·Tx) − 1)·Sf(Z,D) + b·X·ISf(D)]·exp(b·Ty) initiation, niche survival Q3 = exp(b·Ty) − 1 Q4 = exp(b·(Tx + Ty)) − 1

X is radiation-induced initiation, Y is promotion, Z the carrying capacity of a stem-cell niche, and α and β the cell-killing terms. Tx is age at exposure and Ty years since. For a fractionated course, the surviving stem cells regrow logistically between fractions, dn/dt = λ·n·(1 − n/m), and both Sf and ISf are evaluated over the whole schedule. With a single fraction the whole thing collapses back to the acute expressions, which is the check used to verify this implementation.

Lung parameters: X = 0.860 y/Gy, Y = 0.387 /Gy, δ = 9.49 × 10⁻³ /y, log₁₀Z = 4.99, α = 0.25 /Gy, β = 0.025 /Gy², λ = 0.05 /day, b = 0.460 /y.

Source. Shuryak I, Hahnfeldt P, Hlatky L, Sachs RK, Brenner DJ. A new view of radiation-induced cancer: integrating short- and long-term processes. Radiat Environ Biophys 48(3):263–274 (Part I) and 48(3):275–286 (Part II), 2009. Parameters from Part II, Tables 1 and 2.

How life survives extreme radiation

A dose of 5 Gy kills a human. Deinococcus radiodurans shrugs off 12,000 Gy. For decades the assumption was that it must have extraordinary DNA repair. It does not: its genome is shattered into hundreds of fragments like anyone else's.

What it has is chemistry. Small-molecule manganese antioxidant complexes protect the proteins, including the repair enzymes, so the repair machinery still works after the genome has been destroyed. Protect the mechanic, and the car gets rebuilt.

Drag the defense to zero. Survival collapses to a plain exponential and D₁₀ falls from about 12,000 Gy to about 230 Gy. The entire shoulder of the curve, the thing that makes this organism famous, is the inducible antioxidant defense. Nothing about DNA repair changed.

One law, twenty-one organisms, from bacteria to human cells. The x-axis is how many DNA double-strand breaks a cell can survive; the y-axis is how much of its manganese sits in small-molecule antioxidant complexes, measured by electron paramagnetic resonance. A single Hill curve fits the lot (R² = 0.78). Now look at the ringed point, top right. It is not one organism but three: Deinococcus radiodurans, its sodA⁻ knockout, and Deinococcus geothermalis, tabulated identically and plotted on top of one another. Knocking out superoxide dismutase, the enzyme everyone assumed was responsible, changes nothing at all. That is the point.

Where the dose actually goes

Radioligand therapy delivers radiation from inside the body. The tumor dose is not set by a machine, it is set by how long the radiopharmaceutical lingers, which means the absorbed dose is the area under the retention curve. Get the shape of that curve wrong and you get the dose wrong.

The standard approach fits one or two exponentials, which is the same as assuming the body has one or two well-mixed compartments each clearing at a single rate. Real tissue is not like that: uptake and clearance vary continuously from cell to cell. Modeling that spread directly, instead of forcing it into compartments, changes the area under the curve.

On these logarithmic axes a single exponential is a straight line falling off a cliff. The continuous-rate models bend: a population of clearance rates always leaves a slow tail, because the slowest compartments are the last ones standing. That tail is where the absorbed dose hides.

The consequence. For blood, averaging across the models the data actually support gives a time integral about 12% larger than the two-compartment fit alone. Since absorbed dose is proportional to that integral, committing to a compartment model because it is conventional quietly understates the dose. In a therapy whose whole safety case rests on the dose to normal tissue, that is not a rounding error.

A stretched exponential is what you get when the clearance rate is not one number but a distribution: integrate a gamma-distributed rate out of a simple exponential and a power-law tail falls out. This is the same mathematical move that produces the overdispersed survival curve in the dose-response explorable above. Two apparently unrelated problems, one construction.

What is one CT scan actually worth?

Hundreds of millions of CT scans are performed every year, and the standard way to describe their risk is a lifetime cancer risk computed for a healthy person of the patient's age. But the person in the scanner is usually not healthy. That is why they are being scanned.

Radiation-induced cancer takes decades to appear. A patient who will not live those decades cannot express the risk. Ignoring that does not make the estimate conservative, it makes it wrong, and it can frighten patients away from a scan they need.

Compare the two colon-cancer patients. Same age, same scan, same dose. For the patient with early disease the nominal risk estimate is close to right, overstating it by only about 8%. For the patient with metastatic disease it overstates the risk by about 92%: the radiation risk has all but vanished, because the competing risk has not.

Now compare the two bypass patients, because this one is counterintuitive. The younger patient gets the bigger correction, not the older one. A 55-year-old after bypass surgery has lost a great deal of life expectancy relative to a healthy 55-year-old, so the adjustment is large. By 75, a healthy person's remaining life is short anyway, so having had bypass surgery changes the comparison much less.

Show the math

The published quantity is a ratio: this patient's lifetime radiation-associated cancer risk, divided by the risk for a healthy person of the same age and sex. Both are lifetime integrals of the excess risk weighted by the probability of surviving to express it, using empirical life tables and disease-specific survival curves rather than a closed-form model. The radiation dose and the dose-and-dose-rate effectiveness factor cancel out of the ratio entirely, which is why the answer is a pure percentage and does not depend on how big the scan was.

For a 65-year-old having coronary CT angiography after bypass surgery, the published ratio is 0.58 (95% CI 0.52 to 0.64).

Because the underlying life tables are empirical, there is nothing here to re-derive in a browser: the scenarios above are the ones the paper reports, and nothing is interpolated between them.
